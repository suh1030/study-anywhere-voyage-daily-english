W06D1 | Let's Eat: Food Preferences and Habits
  Part 1: Part 1 — What We Eat and Why
    P1L1: I realized I eat the same five or six meals on rotation. No variety at all.
    P1L2: That's actually very common. Research shows most people eat fewer than fifteen different meals regularly.
    P1L3: Fifteen max? That seems way too low. What drives such narrow eating habits?
    P1L4: Familiarity is comforting. We gravitate toward the known, especially when stressed or tired.
    P1L5: Decision fatigue too. By dinner after a long day, the last thing I want is to think about what to eat.
    P1L6: That's why meal planning helps. You make the decision on Sunday so you don't have to make it on Wednesday. [vocab: meal planning]
    P1L7: Have you always been a planning type? Or did you develop that habit?
    P1L8: Developed it. When I lived alone for the first time I wasted a lot of food and money. Necessity taught me to plan.
  Part 2: Part 2 — Picky Eaters and Food Adventurers
    P2L1: Are you a picky eater? I used to be terrible as a kid. Refused vegetables for years. [vocab: picky eater]
    P2L2: I was the opposite — I'd try anything. My parents encouraged adventurous eating from very young.
    P2L3: What changed for you? Most picky kids don't just suddenly start eating everything.
    P2L4: Travel changed me. When you're abroad and the food is what it is, you either eat or go hungry.
    P2L5: Ha! Hunger is a great seasoning. Everything tastes better when you're starving.
    P2L6: And context. Eating street food in a bustling market is completely different from eating the same thing from a supermarket shelf.
    P2L7: Atmosphere really is part of the flavor. That's why restaurant food tastes better than the same recipe at home sometimes.
    P2L8: Expectations, company, ambiance — they all contribute to what your brain registers as taste. [vocab: ambiance]
  Part 3: Part 3 — Comfort Foods
    P3L1: What's your ultimate comfort food? The thing you reach for when you're sick or sad?
    P3L2: Congee without question. My mom's congee specifically — plain with ginger and a little soy sauce on top.
    P3L3: There's always a warmth memory connected to comfort food. It's rarely about the food alone.
    P3L4: Exactly. The congee tastes like being taken care of. Like my mother's hands are in the bowl somehow. [vocab: congee tastes]
    P3L5: For me it's instant noodles. I know it's terrible nutritionally but something about those simple noodles just hits differently.
    P3L6: Instant noodles are one of the world's most beloved comfort foods. The umami, the warmth, the ease.
    P3L7: There's no shame in simple food. Sophistication is not the point of every meal.
    P3L8: Agreed. The function of comfort food is emotional restoration, not nutritional optimization.
  Part 4: Part 4 — Food and Identity
    P4L1: I think a lot about how food defines identity. When I travel, I feel most "Taiwanese" around food.
    P4L2: Diaspora communities often preserve their food traditions more fiercely than anything else. It's a root that travels with you.
    P4L3: My cousin in London has a full Taiwanese kitchen setup. She hunts for specific ingredients across the city. [vocab: ingredients]
    P4L4: Because the taste of home is irreplaceable. You can adjust language, habits, even values — but you don't want to let go of the food.
    P4L5: There's research showing that cultural food keeps immigrant families emotionally grounded.
    P4L6: A bowl of soup can carry an entire heritage in it. It's not trivial. It's deeply significant.
    P4L7: And the reverse — when people are curious about your food, they're being curious about you.
    P4L8: Food as an entry point into culture. Sometimes you can reach someone through their stomach when nothing else works. [vocab: entry point]
  Part 5: Part 5 — Building Better Eating Habits
    P5L1: What changes have you made to your eating habits over the years that actually stuck?
    P5L2: Eating more slowly. I used to rush through meals. Now I put the fork down between bites.
    P5L3: That's harder than it sounds. Especially at the desk. I eat while reading email constantly.
    P5L4: Distracted eating is a real issue. When your attention is split, you eat more without noticing and enjoy it less.
    P5L5: I tried a phone-free lunch rule for a week. It was uncomfortable at first — I didn't know what to do with myself.
    P5L6: That discomfort is revealing. We've lost the ability to simply sit with a meal.
    P5L7: After that week though, I noticed I actually tasted what I was eating. Food got more interesting.
    P5L8: Presence at the table is a form of self-care. The meal deserves your full attention — you deserve to enjoy it.

W06D2 | Eating Out and Restaurant Culture
  Part 1: Part 1 — Why We Love Eating Out
    P1L1: I probably eat out four or five times a week. It's expensive but I can't seem to stop.
    P1L2: I think eating out serves multiple needs at once. Convenience, socialization, and novelty — all in one place.
    P1L3: And you don't have to do the dishes. That's honestly a big selling point for me.
    P1L4: Ha! The full experience — someone brings you food, it appears like magic, and the cleanup disappears too.
    P1L5: And restaurants create an atmosphere you can't replicate at home. The lighting, the music, the energy of other people. [vocab: replicate]
    P1L6: The "stagecraft" of a good restaurant is underappreciated. Interior design, staff training, even the spacing of tables — it all matters.
    P1L7: I never thought of a restaurant as a designed performance space. But it really is.
    P1L8: You're paying for the experience as much as the food. The best ones understand both sides of that equation.
  Part 2: Part 2 — Dining Etiquette
    P2L1: Do you think about table manners much? Things like not putting your elbows on the table?
    P2L2: The elbow rule is old and honestly pretty meaningless now. But some etiquette still matters — like not interrupting a waiter mid-sentence.
    P2L3: How you treat servers says a lot about someone's character. I always pay attention to that on dates.
    P2L4: Absolutely. Rudeness to service staff is a real red flag. It shows entitlement and lack of empathy.
    P2L5: And tipping culture varies so much. In Taiwan we don't really tip. In the US it's almost mandatory. [vocab: mandatory]
    P2L6: The tipping system is controversial because it means servers' income depends on customer generosity rather than a fair wage.
    P2L7: When I travel and the culture does tip, I always do. It's basic respect for local norms. [vocab: local norms]
    P2L8: Good rule. Cultural humility at the dining table. When in Rome, eat as the Romans eat.
  Part 3: Part 3 — Choosing a Restaurant
    P3L1: How do you choose where to eat? Do you use apps, ask friends, just walk in somewhere?
    P3L2: A mix. I check reviews but I don't trust them blindly. Star ratings can be gamed. I read the actual text.
    P3L3: What do you look for in the text? Anything specific?
    P3L4: How people describe the food specifically. Generic "great food great service" tells me nothing. Specific details tell me more.
    P3L5: Also the negative reviews can reveal useful things. Not every complaint is valid, but patterns matter.
    P3L6: Exactly. If five reviews mention "slow service" that's probably real. If one person hated the owner's attitude, that might be noise.
    P3L7: I also ask colleagues. Especially people who eat out a lot. They're usually reliable local guides.
    P3L8: Word of mouth beats algorithms for local restaurants. Personal recommendations carry weight reviews don't.
  Part 4: Part 4 — Business Dining and Work Lunches
    P4L1: Work lunches are a whole different category. You're half eating, half networking, half performing. [vocab: networking]
    P4L2: Three halves — and none of them get your full attention. That's exhausting.
    P4L3: There's something about sitting across from someone and eating that creates intimacy. It works for business too.
    P4L4: Sharing food lowers defenses. You're in a physically vulnerable position — you can't run anywhere, your hands are occupied.
    P4L5: That's an interesting way to think about it. Shared meals as environments of enforced trust.
    P4L6: In many cultures, you don't discuss business until after the meal is done. The eating comes first.
    P4L7: My boss always orders for the table without asking. Is that a power move or just confidence? [vocab: power move]
    P4L8: Depends on the context and how everyone feels about it! If people enjoy it, it's confidence. If they hate it, it's control.
  Part 5: Part 5 — Solo Dining
    P5L1: Do you ever eat out alone? I feel awkward doing that sometimes. [vocab: feel awkward]
    P5L2: All the time. I actually love solo dining. You can fully focus on the food and observe everything around you.
    P5L3: I always feel like people are looking at me and thinking "why is that person alone?"
    P5L4: Most people are too absorbed in their own meals and conversations to notice you. That's the spotlight effect — we overestimate how much others notice us.
    P5L5: I've heard of that! We think we're center stage but we're actually extras in everyone else's story.
    P5L6: Exactly. Try it — take a book or just sit with your thoughts. A solo lunch is actually quite restorative.
    P5L7: I might try it next week. Just me and a bowl of ramen and no phone.
    P5L8: That sounds perfect. Good food, quiet mind. That's really all you need sometimes.

W06D3 | Street Food and Local Eats
  Part 1: Part 1 — The Street Food Experience
    P1L1: I went to the night market last night and spent way too much on stinky tofu and oyster noodles. [vocab: stinky tofu]
    P1L2: Worth every cent though. Night market food hits different — the air, the crowd, the sizzling sounds.
    P1L3: There's something about eating standing up by a stall that feels incredibly free.
    P1L4: No formality. No reservation. No dress code. Just you and the food and the moment.
    P1L5: And the price! Incredible food for almost nothing. The economics of street food are amazing.
    P1L6: Low overhead — no rent for a dining room, no waitstaff. The savings go into the ingredients and the recipe. [vocab: overhead]
    P1L7: Some of the world's most celebrated street food vendors have been doing the same dish for thirty years.
    P1L8: Specialization and mastery. They do one thing and do it better than anyone. That focus is rare.
  Part 2: Part 2 — Hidden Gems and Local Knowledge
    P2L1: Do you have any secret spots you only go to with people you really trust?
    P2L2: Ha! There's a breakfast place near my old apartment that has no sign. You have to know someone who knows. [vocab: old apartment]
    P2L3: The no-sign places are often the best. If you don't need marketing, the food speaks for itself.
    P2L4: That's local knowledge — the kind you only get by living somewhere or knowing locals. Apps can't give you that.
    P2L5: When I travel I always ask hotel staff or cab drivers where they actually eat. Not the tourist list.
    P2L6: That's the move. The places they eat are where the real culture lives. Often cheaper and more authentic.
    P2L7: Authenticity is a strange concept though. Who gets to decide what's "real" local food? [vocab: authenticity]
    P2L8: Good point. All cuisines are always evolving and borrowing from each other. "Authentic" is often just "old enough."
  Part 3: Part 3 — The Social Life of Street Food
    P3L1: Street food is democratic in a way restaurants aren't. Everyone eats at the same stall regardless of income.
    P3L2: That's one of my favorite things about night markets. Executives and students eating side by side.
    P3L3: You don't need to "belong" to a certain income bracket to enjoy great food. It levels the playing field.
    P3L4: And the physical environment encourages interaction. Strangers share benches, make small talk, borrow the chili sauce.
    P3L5: Night markets are like the last outdoor public living room. A community space that's not commercialized. [vocab: commercialized]
    P3L6: Though they are changing. More international brands moving in. The character gets diluted sometimes.
    P3L7: The old stalls getting replaced by chain food is a real loss. The old vendors carry generations of knowledge.
    P3L8: And once a vendor retires without passing on the recipe, that dish is gone forever. No database stores that.
  Part 4: Part 4 — Hygiene and Trust at Street Stalls
    P4L1: Some people are nervous about street food hygiene. Is that fear valid or overblown?
    P4L2: Depends on the context. A stall that's been operating for twenty years with a queue out the door is probably fine.
    P4L3: The crowd is the clue. If locals line up for it, it's survived their scrutiny for years.
    P4L4: High turnover also helps — when food moves quickly, it's not sitting around getting old.
    P4L5: I've gotten sick from fancy restaurants more than street stalls, honestly.
    P4L6: Price doesn't correlate directly with safety. A lot of high-end places prep food hours ahead. [vocab: correlate]
    P4L7: That's a good point. The stall cooking fresh to order is often safer than refrigerated and reheated.
    P4L8: Trust your senses and look for the crowds. Those are the two most reliable indicators.
  Part 5: Part 5 — Street Food as Cultural Tourism
    P5L1: When I visit a new city, the first thing I want is to find its food scene. Start with the local street food.
    P5L2: Me too. You can read a hundred pages about a place and still miss its character. But eat the local food and something clicks.
    P5L3: It's experiential knowledge. You understand the pace of life through the street food.
    P5L4: What people eat for breakfast reveals their morning tempo. Fast and on the go, or slow and seated. It says a lot.
    P5L5: Japanese breakfast culture versus American grab-and-go are almost opposite philosophies.
    P5L6: And both reflect broader values. Japan: presentation, seasonality, ritual. America: efficiency, individualism, convenience.
    P5L7: So street food is actually a compressed version of a culture's values. That's remarkable.
    P5L8: You can learn more about a society from what it eats and how it eats than from almost anything else.

W06D4 | Healthy Eating and Diets
  Part 1: Part 1 — The Diet Culture Problem
    P1L1: I feel like every few years there's a new diet that everyone is doing. Keto, intermittent fasting, plant-based... [vocab: intermittent fasting]
    P1L2: The cycle of diet trends is almost predictable. They emerge, become trendy, get debunked, fade out.
    P1L3: And then the same principles get rebranded and come back. "Clean eating" today was "diet food" in the 90s.
    P1L4: The food industry benefits from confusion. If people aren't sure what's healthy, they buy more products promising to help.
    P1L5: That's a bit cynical but probably true. Nutritional science keeps changing its recommendations too.
    P1L6: Because it's a young science and diet is incredibly complex. Individual variation matters enormously.
    P1L7: What works for one person can be terrible for another. That's the frustrating part.
    P1L8: Which is why "eat what makes you feel good and gives you energy" is probably better advice than any specific diet.
  Part 2: Part 2 — What Healthy Actually Means
    P2L1: How do you define healthy eating for yourself? Not in a textbook way.
    P2L2: Energy stability throughout the day. No big crashes. Sleeping well. Feeling clear-headed.
    P2L3: Output-based definition rather than input-based. Not "I ate X grams of protein" but "how do I feel?"
    P2L4: Exactly. Your body gives you feedback constantly. The problem is we're not trained to listen to it.
    P2L5: I used to eat lunch and then feel like sleeping at my desk every afternoon. Turns out I was eating too many carbs.
    P2L6: The post-lunch slump is often a blood sugar spike and crash. Protein and fiber stabilize that. [vocab: blood sugar spike]
    P2L7: Once I switched to a higher-protein lunch, the crash stopped. That was more motivating than any diet book.
    P2L8: Personal experiment is the best nutrition research. You are your own most relevant subject.
  Part 3: Part 3 — Plant-Based Eating
    P3L1: Plant-based diets seem to be the current consensus for health and environment. Do you eat much meat? [vocab: plant-based]
    P3L2: Less than I used to. I haven't gone fully vegetarian, but I try to have a few meat-free days each week.
    P3L3: Flexitarian. Neither fully vegetarian nor fully omnivore.
    P3L4: That seems like the most sustainable approach for most people. Perfect can be the enemy of good. [vocab: sustainable]
    P3L5: Some people feel guilty about not being fully plant-based, so they give up entirely instead of doing better within their means.
    P3L6: All-or-nothing thinking is the biggest enemy of behavior change. Incremental is real.
    P3L7: I replaced one beef meal a week with tofu and honestly can't tell a huge difference. Steps like that add up.
    P3L8: That's the 80/20 of it. Small consistent changes compound over time. No dramatic overhaul needed.
  Part 4: Part 4 — Food and Mental Health
    P4L1: There's increasingly more research on how food affects mood. The gut-brain connection. [vocab: gut-brain connection]
    P4L2: The gut is called the "second brain" for good reason. Most serotonin is produced there, not in your head.
    P4L3: So when I eat poorly and feel grumpy and foggy, it might literally be my gut responding.
    P4L4: Very likely. Ultra-processed food is linked to higher rates of depression and anxiety in large studies.
    P4L5: That's not about weight — it's about neurochemistry. How food affects your brain directly.
    P4L6: Fermented foods in particular seem to support gut bacteria in ways that affect mood. Kimchi, yogurt, miso.
    P4L7: I've been eating more miso soup and I do notice I feel better. Hard to say if it's placebo though.
    P4L8: Even if it's partly placebo, the positive effect is real. The mechanism matters less than the outcome.
  Part 5: Part 5 — A Balanced Relationship with Food
    P5L1: I think the healthiest thing is having a relaxed relationship with food rather than being obsessive about it.
    P5L2: Absolutely. Orthorexia — the obsession with eating healthily — is itself a disorder. Too much focus on food is unhealthy.
    P5L3: So eating the "wrong" food sometimes is fine. The stress of restriction is its own harm.
    P5L4: Cortisol from chronic stress actually causes more harm than most "bad" foods. Enjoyment has biological value. [vocab: chronic stress]
    P5L5: Eating a piece of cake with friends while laughing is probably better for you than eating a "perfect" meal alone and anxious.
    P5L6: The social and emotional context of eating is a legitimate part of nutrition. We've lost that dimension in modern diet culture.
    P5L7: So eat mostly whole foods, don't stress about occasional indulgences, and eat with people when you can.
    P5L8: That's basically it. Ancient wisdom, modern framing. Nothing complicated about a joyful relationship with food.

W06D5 | Food Memories and Family Recipes
  Part 1: Part 1 — Taste as Memory
    P1L1: I had a soup last week that tasted exactly like something my grandma made. I nearly cried at the restaurant table. [vocab: nearly cried]
    P1L2: That involuntary rush of memory is called the Proust phenomenon. A taste or smell can unlock a very specific moment in your past.
    P1L3: Named after the writer who wrote about a madeleine cookie triggering childhood memories, right?
    P1L4: Exactly. The olfactory system — smell — has a direct pathway to the hippocampus, the memory center. No other sense has that shortcut.
    P1L5: So smelling my grandma's soup literally activates the same neural pathways as being in her kitchen as a child.
    P1L6: Yes. It's why food memory is so emotionally potent. The food and the memory literally share the same neural real estate.
    P1L7: That means cooking your grandmother's recipes is a way of visiting her, in a way.
    P1L8: I think that's exactly what's happening. The kitchen becomes a time machine. The dish becomes a portal.
  Part 2: Part 2 — Preserving Family Recipes
    P2L1: My aunt makes a fermented pork dish that nobody else knows how to make. I'm terrified it'll be lost.
    P2L2: Have you asked her to teach you? Or at least to let you film her making it?
    P2L3: I keep meaning to but never do. That's a regret waiting to happen.
    P2L4: Do it now. Seriously. Older relatives' health can change quickly. The window can close faster than you expect.
    P2L5: You're right. The recipe isn't written down anywhere. It lives only in her hands and memory.
    P2L6: Oral recipe traditions are incredibly fragile. One generation is all it takes to lose centuries of knowledge.
    P2L7: I'll call her this week and set up a cooking session. Bring a notebook and camera.
    P2L8: Perfect. And ask the stories behind the dish too, not just the steps. That's the part that makes it come alive.
  Part 3: Part 3 — Recreating Food from Memory
    P3L1: Have you ever tried to recreate a dish from memory? Something you ate once and can't forget? [vocab: recreate]
    P3L2: Yes! A lamb stew I had in a mountain village during a trip. I've made fifteen versions trying to get it right.
    P3L3: Fifteen! And did any of them capture it?
    P3L4: Close but not quite. I think the altitude, the wood fire, the person who made it — those things can't be replicated in my kitchen.
    P3L5: So memory is also context. The dish existed in that specific moment, with those people, in that air.
    P3L6: The search itself has value though. I've become a much better cook from those fifteen experiments.
    P3L7: The memory is a guide, not a destination. You're not really trying to recreate the dish — you're having a conversation with it.
    P3L8: That's a beautiful way to put it. Cooking as dialogue with the past. [vocab: dialogue]
  Part 4: Part 4 — Adapting Old Recipes for New Times
    P4L1: Is it okay to change traditional family recipes? Or does that break the chain?
    P4L2: Change is inevitable. My grandfather's recipes used ingredients I can't find anymore. You adapt or you lose the dish entirely.
    P4L3: Substituting ingredients while keeping the spirit. Not the exact formula but the feeling.
    P4L4: And sometimes the adaptation is an improvement. You find a better technique or a complementary ingredient.
    P4L5: My mom adapted grandma's soup to be vegetarian because she developed a dairy allergy. Same warmth, different ingredients. [vocab: dairy allergy]
    P4L6: That's adaptation in service of continuity. The most important thing survived — the ritual and the warmth.
    P4L7: Would grandma mind? Probably a little. But she'd mind more if the dish disappeared entirely.
    P4L8: Recipes are vessels, not sacred relics. What matters is the love and care that travels inside them.
  Part 5: Part 5 — Creating New Food Memories
    P5L1: Do you consciously try to create food memories now? Knowing that a meal can become someone's Proust moment. [vocab: consciously]
    P5L2: Somewhat. When I have people over for a special occasion, I want the meal to be memorable. But you can't force it.
    P5L3: The best memories happen in the margins. Not the formal dinner, but the late-night snack you shared after.
    P5L4: Exactly. Or the rushed breakfast before a journey. Or the takeout eaten on the floor because there were no chairs yet.
    P5L5: Those imperfect, unplanned meals are sometimes the ones that stick the most.
    P5L6: Because emotion does the encoding. High emotion — laughter, surprise, love — burns the memory in deeper. [vocab: encoding]
    P5L7: So the secret to creating lasting food memories isn't the food at all — it's the emotional intensity of the moment.
    P5L8: Yes. Be fully present, genuinely connected. That's how ordinary meals become extraordinary memories.

W06D6 | International Cuisine and Fusion
  Part 1: Part 1 — The World on Your Plate
    P1L1: I walked past four completely different cuisines in one city block today. Japanese, Thai, Italian, and Peruvian.
    P1L2: That's globalization on a plate. Fifty years ago that would have been unimaginable in most cities.
    P1L3: The variety is incredible, but does it change the cuisine? A Thai restaurant in a foreign country is never quite the same.
    P1L4: Adaptation is inevitable. You use local ingredients, accommodate local palates. The cuisine evolves in transit.
    P1L5: Sometimes the exported version becomes its own thing. American Chinese food is nothing like food in China, but it's its own valid cuisine.
    P1L6: Each migration creates a new culinary tradition. It's a living record of where people moved and how they adapted.
    P1L7: I never thought of food as a form of migration history. But that's exactly what it is. [vocab: migration history]
    P1L8: Follow the spice routes, follow the movement of peoples. The food tells the story.
  Part 2: Part 2 — The Fusion Debate
    P2L1: Fusion food gets a bad reputation sometimes. "Not authentic," people say. [vocab: fusion food]
    P2L2: The criticism is often about execution, not the concept. Bad fusion mixes things randomly without understanding either cuisine.
    P2L3: Good fusion requires deep knowledge of both traditions. You have to understand the rules before you break them.
    P2L4: Exactly. A chef who combines Taiwanese and Japanese flavors successfully probably knows both cuisines inside out.
    P2L5: And culturally — is fusion ever appropriation? Or is it always celebration?
    P2L6: That's a complex question. Intent and context matter. Are you honoring the source culture or commodifying it?
    P2L7: Giving credit, learning seriously, sharing profits fairly — those seem like the markers of respectful cross-cultural cooking.
    P2L8: Agreed. The world's most interesting cuisines have always been the result of cross-cultural exchange anyway. [vocab: exchange]
  Part 3: Part 3 — Trying Unfamiliar Foods
    P3L1: What's the most unfamiliar or challenging food you've ever eaten?
    P3L2: I tried a fermented shark from Iceland. The smell was almost unbearable but I got through it. One small piece.
    P3L3: Ha! That's notorious for being one of the world's most challenging foods. How did it taste? [vocab: notorious]
    P3L4: Like a very strong blue cheese crossed with ammonia. Not pleasant, but I'm glad I tried.
    P3L5: The act of trying matters even if you don't like it. You learn your own limits and expand your reference point.
    P3L6: And it's respectful. When someone offers you their traditional food and you try it, you're acknowledging its value.
    P3L7: The refusal to try can feel like a small rejection of the person offering. Food as connection again.
    P3L8: Yes. A willingness to try someone's food is a willingness to try understanding them. That goes a long way.
  Part 4: Part 4 — Cooking Internationally at Home
    P4L1: I've been trying to cook one new international recipe a week. Last week was Indian dal. The week before, Greek moussaka.
    P4L2: That's a great habit! You're learning culinary geography from your own kitchen. What have you discovered?
    P4L3: How much overlap there is. The spice combinations, the cooking techniques — so many parallels across different cultures.
    P4L4: Because humans solved similar problems in similar ways. Preserve protein, add flavor, add warmth. The specific ingredients vary but the logic is universal. [vocab: preserve]
    P4L5: Slow cooking meat until tender appears in almost every cuisine. The technique is the same, the seasoning changes.
    P4L6: Braising, stewing, pot roasting — different names, same beautiful principle. Low and slow.
    P4L7: I'm starting to see cooking as universal language. Different vocabularies, same grammar. [vocab: universal language]
    P4L8: Perfectly put. And every time you cook a new cuisine, you learn a new vocabulary. The grammar was always the same.
  Part 5: Part 5 — Food as Diplomacy
    P5L1: Could food actually solve international conflicts? I know that sounds naive, but there's something to it.
    P5L2: Gastrodiplomacy is a real thing. Thailand has funded Thai restaurants globally as a soft power strategy.
    P5L3: The idea that "I love your food" can become "I respect your culture" which can become "I want to understand you."
    P5L4: Exactly. You can't easily hate a people whose food you love. It humanizes the abstract.
    P5L5: Is that why exchange programs always involve food? Let someone cook for you and you see them differently.
    P5L6: Cooking for someone is an act of care and vulnerability. It opens people up in ways formal meetings can't.
    P5L7: So the path to world peace runs through the kitchen? I'm only half joking.
    P5L8: Ha! Maybe the other half isn't a joke. Shared meals, shared humanity. It's not the worst starting point.

W06D7 | Food Trends and the Future of Eating
  Part 1: Part 1 — Where Food Is Heading
    P1L1: I read that lab-grown meat could be commercially viable within the next decade. How do you feel about that?
    P1L2: Genuinely curious about it. If it tastes the same and the environmental footprint is lower, it makes sense.
    P1L3: The "yuck factor" though. Psychologically, eating something grown in a lab feels different from something raised on a farm. [vocab: yuck factor]
    P1L4: That reaction is cultural, not rational. Sushi was once considered revolting in the West. Perceptions change.
    P1L5: Fair point. What about insect protein? I've seen cricket flour in a health food store.
    P1L6: Two billion people globally already eat insects regularly. It's Western culture that has the aversion.
